Why Adult Sex Ratio Matters

Ungulate management depends on population structure

  • ASR influences harvest quotas, population growth projections, and habitat management
  • Sex-biased browsing pressure affects forest regeneration
  • Camera traps are cost-effective but imperfect sensors

Important

The problem: Not every sighting can be sexed. Unknown-sex sightings may bias ASR estimates if ignored or handled incorrectly.

The ASR Equation

\[\text{ASR} = \frac{N_{\text{males}}}{N_{\text{males}} + N_{\text{females}}}\]

  • ASR = 0.5 → equal numbers of males and females
  • ASR < 0.5 → female-biased (common in polygynous ungulates)
  • ASR > 0.5 → male-biased

Key question: What do we do with sightings where sex is unknown?

Three Approaches to ASR

Approach 1: Naive ASR

Simple: Use only sightings where sex is identified

\[\text{ASR}_{\text{naive}} = \frac{N_{\text{males, known}}}{N_{\text{males, known}} + N_{\text{females, known}}}\]

Assumption: Unknown-sex sightings are missing completely at random (MCAR)

When this fails:

  • Males may be easier to identify (antlers) → unknowns skew female
  • Camera placement may favor one sex
  • Unknown rate varies by season or camera location

Approach 2: Predicted ASR (GAM)

Idea: Predict sex for unknown sightings using a statistical model

Model: Generalized Additive Model (binomial, logit link)

\[\text{logit}(P(\text{male})) = f_1(\text{day\_of\_year}) + f_2(\text{hour}) + s(\text{camera\_id}) + f_3(\text{dist\_road} \times \text{day\_of\_year})\]

Prediction rule: If \(P(\text{male}) > 0.5\) → classify as male; otherwise female

Performance: Precision ≈ 75.24%; Deviance explained ≈ 29.5%

Approach 3: Occupancy-Based ASR (Royle–Nichols)

Key insight: Detection probability depends on abundance

\[P(\text{detect} \geq 1 \mid N, r) = 1 - (1-r)^N\]

  • \(N\) = latent abundance at a site (Poisson distributed)
  • \(r\) = per-individual detection probability

For ASR: Fit RN model separately for males and females

\[\text{ASR}_{\text{occupancy}} = \frac{\bar{N}_{\text{male}}}{\bar{N}_{\text{male}} + \bar{N}_{\text{female}}}\]

Requires: Detection histories (sites × weekly replicates), closure assumption

What Did the Paper Find?

Year Naive ASR Predicted ASR Occupancy ASR
2020 0.40 (0.34–0.46) 0.44 (0.39–0.49) 0.40 (0.34–0.47)
2021 0.40 (0.31–0.49) 0.46 (0.39–0.54) 0.36 (0.36–0.36)
2022 0.42 (0.35–0.50) 0.48 (0.41–0.56) 0.45 (0.44–0.46)

Bottom line: Under these conditions (~8.9% unknowns, trail cameras), all three methods gave comparable results.

But: This does NOT mean naive is always fine. When unknowns are >20–30% or cameras are non-randomly placed, advanced methods become essential.
