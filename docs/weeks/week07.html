Week 07 - Your analysis

Week: 8

Topic: The Philosophy Behind Your Analysis

1 Week Overview

Session Content
Session A Case-based philosophy + peer review (~75 min)
Session B Workshop: project progress, peer review & method justification (~75 min)
Note

No student presenter is available this week. Come ready to read, diagnose, and argue.

1.1 Learning Objectives

  1. Connect the philosophical ideas from Weeks 2, 3, and 5 to to someone else’s flawed analysis before your own
  2. Articulate and defend your method choice in writing and verbally
  3. Identify common philosophical pitfalls by diagnosing them in a real scenario
  4. Assess your project’s current progress against your planned timeline

2.3 Part 2: The Case — “Dr. Reyes’s Grassland Study” (20 min)

2.3.1 Setup (3 min)

You are a peer reviewer for the journal Ecological Applications. You’ve just received the following methods section. Your job: find everything that’s wrong — and explain why it matters.

📄 The Manuscript Excerpt — Read Carefully

Title: Nitrogen addition increases aboveground biomass in semi-arid grasslands

Methods (excerpt):

We established 12 plots across 4 ranches in eastern Colorado. Each ranch received one of three nitrogen treatments: control (0 kg N/ha), low (25 kg N/ha), and high (50 kg N/ha). Biomass was harvested from each plot at three time points: May, July, and September of the same year.

To assess treatment effects, we calculated mean biomass per treatment per time point and ran a one-way ANOVA (treatment as the fixed effect) on the September harvest only, as this represented “peak biomass.” We report p-values and conclude that high nitrogen significantly increased biomass (p = 0.03). All plots were located within 5 km of one another on each ranch.

We selected ANOVA because it is the standard method in grassland ecology and because our dataset was small. We acknowledge that data were not fully independent but considered this a minor concern.

Individual choice of reject, revisions or accept.

2.4 Part 3: Mini-Lecture — Why Philosophy Matters for YOUR Analysis (20 min)

2.4.1 Recap: Key Philosophical Ideas from Previous Weeks

Week Idea Implication for Your Work
Week 2 “All models are wrong, some are useful” (Box) Your model doesn’t need to be true — it needs to be defensible
Week 2 Bayesian vs. frequentist frameworks Know which framework you’re working in and why
Week 3 Method–Question–Data Triangle If these three don’t align, your results are unreliable
Week 5 Split-plot, mixed models vs. ANOVA Same data can produce multiple analyses; your design dictates the right one

Discuss the paper

2.5 Part 3: 🔬 Case Diagnosis — Small Group Work (20 min)

Work in groups of 3–4. Each group gets one diagnostic role you are responsible for that angle, but your findings feed the whole class.

2.5.1 Role Assignments

Role Your Diagnostic Focus
🔺 Triangle Auditors Does the method match the question, data type, and structure? What would the correct model be?
🧭 Framework Detectives What philosophical framework is Dr. Reyes implicitly using? Where does it break down? What would a Bayesian alternative look like?
✂️ Complexity Judges Was dropping July and May data defensible? What is lost? Is the model too simple — or could it have been simpler in a different direction?
🔁 Reproducibility Auditors What would you need to reproduce this analysis? What is missing? List specific items.

2.5.2 Group Task (12 min)

  1. Read the excerpt again as a group
  2. Prepare a 2-minute verbal finding — one clear diagnosis + one concrete fix
  3. Assign a spokesperson

2.5.3 Whole-Class Report-Out (8 min)

Each group presents. Dr. Molina synthesizes, connects to student discussion interests from the minute papers where relevant, and links findings back to the Triangle, Box, and framework ideas.

Instructor Note: Facilitation Moves
  • Push Triangle Auditors to name the actual R function, not just “a better model”
  • Ask Framework Detectives: “If Dr. Reyes had been a Bayesian, where would the analysis have looked different first?”
  • Ask Complexity Judges: “Is there a version of this that is simpler and more honest at the same time?”
  • Ask Reproducibility Auditors to list what they’d email Dr. Reyes to ask for before reviewing further

2.7 Part 4b: 🟢🟡🔴 Assumptions Audit (10 min)

SoTL Activity #3: Assumptions Traffic Light

Use this immediately after defending your method — while the conversation is still fresh.

Every model makes assumptions. Most people know this. Fewer people have actually checked which ones they’ve met.

On a new sheet or the back of your Method Defense Card, list every assumption your chosen model requires. Then rate each one:

Assumption Status
(e.g., independence of residuals) 🟢 Checked — and it holds
(e.g., normality of random effects) 🟡 Haven’t checked yet — but I could by Thursday
(e.g., no spatial autocorrelation) 🔴 Can’t fully check — need to acknowledge and bound my claims

The goal is not to have all green. Red assumptions are not failures — they are honest limitations that belong in your Discussion section. The failure is not knowing which color you’re at.

2.7.1 Central Question

“Can you defend your analysis to a skeptical reviewer?”

2.7.2 Four Requirements of a Good Defense

  1. Philosophical grounding — What framework are you working in? What assumptions does it require?
  2. Data–method alignment — Does your method match your response variable type and data structure?
  3. Simplicity vs. complexity — Is your model as simple as possible while still being honest?
  4. Transparency — Could someone reproduce your analysis and understand your reasoning?

2.7.3 Three Common Pitfalls

Pitfall 1: Method Chosen by Habit, Not Design

“I’m using ANOVA because my advisor always uses ANOVA.”

Your design may require a GLMM. Habit is not a philosophical defense.

Pitfall 2: Philosophical Mismatch

Using a Bayesian framework but interpreting results with p-values and null hypothesis language.

Know your framework. Bayesian credible intervals and posterior probabilities are not the same as p-values and confidence intervals.

Pitfall 3: The Triangle Is Broken

Repeated measures + count data, but using a simple t-test.

Check all three corners of the triangle — question, data type, and method — every time.

2.7.4 Decision Flowchart: Choosing Your Method

What is your research question?
        |
        |-----> Descriptive / exploratory?
        |               --> Summary statistics, visualization
        |
        |-----> Predictive?
        |               --> Consider cross-validation, predictive modeling
        |
        |-----> Inferential / causal?
                        |
                        |--> What is your response variable?
                        |
                        |-----> Continuous, normal?
                        |               --> lm(), lmer() if structure present
                        |
                        |-----> Counts (integers >= 0)?
                        |               --> glm(Poisson) or glmer(Poisson)
                        |
                        |-----> Proportions / binary?
                        |               --> glm(binomial) or glmer(binomial)
                        |
                        |--> What is your data structure?
                                |
                                |-----> Simple, independent observations?
                                |               --> lm() or glm()
                                |
                                |-----> Blocked / repeated / nested?
                                                --> lmer() or glmer()
Key Takeaway

The best analysis is the simplest one that honestly addresses your question given your data structure.

Complexity must be earned by your data and your question — not borrowed from habit or perceived sophistication.

2.9 Part 4: Philosophical Stations — Small Group Rotation (15 min)

Work in groups of 3–4. ⏱ 5 minutes per station, then rotate.

2.9.1 Station 1 — “The Box Station”

How is your model “wrong”? Why is it still useful?

Write your answer on the station sheet. Be specific — what assumptions does your model violate? Why does it still give you defensible insight?

2.9.2 Station 2 — “The Inference Station”

Is your claim causal, predictive, or descriptive? Does your analysis actually support that type of claim?

Write the type of inference you are making and one sentence explaining how your method supports (or fails to support) it.

2.9.3 Station 3 — “The Reproducibility Station”

Could someone reproduce your analysis from your GitHub repo today?

List two things that are currently missing (data, code, README, environment details, etc.).

Brief whole-class debrief (2–3 min): What patterns did you notice across stations?
