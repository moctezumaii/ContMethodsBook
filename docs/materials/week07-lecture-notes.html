Week 08 — Lecture Notes: The Philosophy Behind Your Analysis

NoteInstructor Use Only

These are structured talking points for the ~20-minute mini-lecture in Session A, Part 2. They are not distributed to students in advance.

1 Talking Points (~20 min)

1.1 Opening Hook (2 min)

“Imagine you’re in your dissertation defense. Your committee asks: ‘Why did you use this method?’ What do you say?”

Pause. Let the question land.

“If your answer is ‘because my advisor does it that way’ or ‘because I Googled it and it seemed right’ — that’s a problem. Not because those methods are wrong, but because you can’t defend them. Today we’re going to practice defending them.”

Transition: “Let’s start by revisiting something from Week 2 that sounds abstract but is deeply practical.”

1.2 Section 1: Revisiting Box (5 min)

Core idea: “All models are wrong, some are useful.” — George Box

This is not a cop-out or a disclaimer. It is a design principle.

What it means in practice:

Your model doesn’t need to perfectly describe reality. It needs to be wrong in a way that doesn’t matter for your specific question.

Two examples of being wrong in a way that DOES matter:

WarningExample 1: Linear model on count data

lm(insect_count ~ treatment)

Count data cannot be negative. A linear model can predict negative counts. When your counts are small (0–10), this matters — your inference is compromised.

Wrong in a way that matters.

WarningExample 2: RCBD ANOVA on a split-plot design

aov(yield ~ variety + block) — ignoring that sowing time is a whole-plot factor.

You are using the wrong error term for the whole-plot factor. Your F-ratio for sowing time is anticonservative (too many false positives).

Wrong in a way that matters. (See Week 5.)

Actionable version for students:

Ask yourself: “In what way is my model wrong? Does that wrongness affect my conclusions?”

If you can answer that honestly, you are doing philosophy.

1.3 Section 2: What Kind of Inference? (5 min)

Core idea: Your method must match the type of claim you want to make.

Type What you’re doing What you need Example
Descriptive Summarizing what is in your data Representative sample, appropriate summary stats “Mean wheat yield was 4.2 Mg/ha across all sites”
Predictive Forecasting new observations Cross-validation, held-out test set “A model trained on 2020–2022 predicts 2023 yield within 0.3 Mg/ha”
Causal Claiming X causes Y Randomization or strong causal design + assumptions “Nitrogen application increased yield by 0.8 Mg/ha” (from RCT)
ImportantWarning: Observational Data + Regression ≠ Causal

This is one of the most common errors in applied agricultural and ecological research.

“We found that farms with higher biodiversity had lower pest pressure (β = −0.42, p < 0.001). Therefore, increasing biodiversity reduces pests.”

This is a causal claim from an observational study. The regression tells you there is an association. It does not tell you why.

You need a randomized experiment, a natural experiment, or explicit causal modeling (DAGs, instrumental variables) to make causal claims.

Transition: “So: what kind of inference are you making? Point at your Methods section mentally. Does your method actually support that type of claim?”

1.4 Section 3: The Triangle in Practice (5 min)

Core idea: Question + Data + Method must all align. (Week 3 recap, with new examples.)

Three concrete examples from ag / natural resources:

TipGood Alignment: Nitrogen Rate × Year on Wheat Yield
  • Question: Does nitrogen rate affect wheat yield, and does this vary by year?
  • Data: Continuous yield (kg/ha); blocked RCT; two years; plots within blocks
  • Method: lmer(yield ~ N_rate * Year + (1|Block), data = dat)
  • Triangle: ✅ Continuous response → lmer; blocked design → random block; factorial treatment → interaction term

This is defensible.

WarningCommon Mistake: Bird Richness Counts with Repeated Measures → aov()
  • Question: Is bird species richness declining over years across urban parks?
  • Data: Integer counts (0–47); 8 parks; 6 visits/year; 5 years; counts are non-normal
  • Method used: aov(richness ~ year)
  • Triangle: ❌ Count data → Poisson, not Gaussian; repeated measures → random effects needed; pseudoreplication ignored

Should be: glmer(richness ~ year + (1|park), family = poisson, data = dat)

NoteOvercomplicated: Soil pH, 2 Groups, 10 Samples → Bayesian Hierarchical Model
  • Question: Does tillage treatment affect soil pH?
  • Data: 10 samples per treatment (n = 20 total); continuous pH; no nesting or grouping structure
  • Method used: Full Bayesian hierarchical model with weakly informative priors and MCMC
  • Triangle: ⚠️ A t-test (or simple Bayesian two-sample comparison) is sufficient. The added complexity provides no additional insight and makes your methods section impenetrable.

Complexity must be earned by your data, not borrowed from methodology papers.

1.5 Section 4: Tying It Together (3 min)

Core message:

“Your methods section is not just a technical description — it is a philosophical argument.”

When a reviewer reads your methods, they are asking:

  1. Did you know what kind of claim you were making?
  2. Did you choose a method that is honest about what your data can support?
  3. Did you avoid being either naively simple or gratuitously complex?

Transition to Think-Pair-Share:

“Now you’re going to make that argument — out loud, to a peer who will push back. Take out a notecard.”
