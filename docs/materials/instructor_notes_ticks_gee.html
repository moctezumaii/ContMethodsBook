Instructor Notes — Tick Phenology & Prescribed Burning: GEE for Repeated-Measures Count Data

SNR 690 — Contemporary Approaches to Quantitative Methods

For Instructor Use Only

This document is not for distribution to students. It provides step-by-step facilitation guidance, expected student difficulties, contingency plans, and follow-up assignments.

1 How to Run the Class

1.1 Pre-Class Setup (~15 min before)

  • Distribute (or post on Canvas) activities/pre_activity_entry_ticket.csv so students have it ready at the start.
  • Open slides/slides-ticks-gee.qmd in your browser — advance past the title slide to Slide 3 (Entry Ticket Debrief prompts) so it is ready when you walk in.
  • Write the Hot-Seat Q&A instructions on the board (or keep Slide 9 ready): “Your group has 60 seconds to write one question for the presenter. The presenter will answer on the spot.”
  • Confirm that the presenter knows the Hot-Seat is coming at ~20 min into their presentation (tell them at the start or remind them the day before).
  • If students will run R code live, verify geepack and MASS are installed in the classroom R environment.

1.2 Minute-by-Minute Facilitation Guide

1.2.1 0:00–0:05 — Entry Ticket (5 min)

  • Project the entry ticket fields on the screen (Slide 3) or tell students to open the CSV.
  • Say: “Five minutes — paper out, answer these. Be honest; this is not graded.”
  • Walk around and read over students’ shoulders. Mentally note:
    • Did students identify plots (not individual ticks) as the clusters?
    • Did anyone mention GEE, repeated measures, or clustering?
    • Any especially good or creative method questions?

1.2.2 0:05–0:10 — Entry Ticket Debrief (5 min)

  • Summarize verbally: “I saw a few people say [X]. That’s exactly right because…” and “Several of you wrote [Y] — let’s come back to that during the lecture.”
  • Key points to reinforce:
    • Cluster = plot (not individual ticks, not year)
    • Outcome = count data (overdispersed → negative binomial is defensible)
    • GEE is appropriate when you want marginal (population-averaged) effects
Tip

Facilitation tip: Don’t give away the full lecture yet. Use this 5 minutes to whet curiosity. Say: “There’s a really interesting tension between GEE and GLMM for this design — we’ll get to that.”

1.2.3 0:10–0:40 — Student Presentation (30 min)

Your role: Active moderator, not passive audience.

  • At minute 12–15 (before the Hot-Seat), listen for whether the presenter:
    • Correctly identifies the cluster structure
    • Mentions overdispersion or count data
    • Explains what GEE does (or tries to)
  • At approximately 20 minutes into the presentation, interrupt politely: “Great — let’s pause here for our Hot-Seat Q&A round.”

1.2.3.1 Facilitating the Hot-Seat (5 min within the presentation)

  1. Announce: “Groups, you have 60 seconds to write one question for [presenter’s name]. This is a question they must answer right now.”
  2. Set a visible timer (phone or projector).
  3. Call on 2–3 groups (time permitting): “Group 1, what’s your question?”
  4. After each question, say: “[Presenter], you have 30 seconds.”
  5. If the presenter struggles, do NOT immediately rescue — wait 5 seconds, then offer: “Anyone want to help [presenter] out?”, and then ask the presenter to synthesize.
  6. After the Hot-Seat, let the presenter finish their remaining ~10 minutes.
Warning

Common presenter mistake: Trying to answer questions with “I think…” followed by a restatement of the paper. Encourage them to use their own words and to explicitly say “I don’t know, but I would look at…” if they are uncertain.

1.2.4 0:40–0:55 — GEE Mini-Lecture (15 min)

Use Slides 4–8. Key teaching points per slide:

  • Slide 4 (Why GEE?): Emphasize marginal vs conditional interpretation. “GEE answers: what is the average effect of burn treatment across all plots?”
  • Slide 5 (Estimating Equation): Walk through each symbol slowly. Spend 2–3 min on \(\mathbf{V}_i = \mathbf{A}_i^{1/2} R(\alpha) \mathbf{A}_i^{1/2}\). Key insight: the working correlation does not have to be correct — GEE is consistent even if \(R(\alpha)\) is wrong.
  • Slide 6 (Sandwich Variance): One sentence: “The sandwich variance is consistent even when the working covariance is wrong — that is GEE’s magic.” Then move on.
  • Slide 7 (Paper’s Model): Be explicit that the paper does NOT give a formal equation. Show the inferred model and ask: “Does anyone want to challenge this specification?”
  • Slide 8 (Working Correlation Table): Spend 2 min — AR(1) is usually more realistic for monthly ecological data, but exchangeable is common in practice. This is a good setup for the group worksheet.

1.2.5 0:55–1:05 — Group Worksheet (10 min)

  • Distribute activities/worksheet_group_ticks.md (or show it on screen).
  • Give explicit instructions: “You have 10 minutes. Answer as many of the 6 prompts as you can — but your main deliverable is one ‘challenge question’ for [presenter’s name] at the end.”
  • Circulate. Suggested probing questions when groups get stuck:
    • “Would the correlation between tick counts in January and December be the same as between January and February? What does that imply?” (prompts AR(1) vs exchangeable thinking)
    • “If they had used a GLMM instead, how would you interpret the burn regime coefficient differently?” (marginal vs conditional)
    • “The paper says ‘negative binomial GEE’ — but geepack::geeglm doesn’t have a negative binomial family option. What does that tell you about the software?”

1.2.6 1:05–1:12 — Group Reports + Challenge Questions (7 min)

  • Call on 2–3 groups: “What is your group’s challenge question for [presenter]?”
  • After each question, give the presenter 45–60 seconds to respond.
  • If responses are weak, ask follow-up: “[Presenter], do you want to revise that answer?”
  • Briefly synthesize after all groups: “Here’s what I want you to take away from these questions…”
Tip

If time is tight: Skip to the most conceptually rich challenge question (usually the GEE vs GLMM one or the AR(1) vs exchangeable one).

1.2.7 1:12–1:15 — Minute Paper (3 min)

  • Project Slide 11 (Minute Paper prompts).
  • Say: “Two questions, index card or phone. You have 3 minutes. Be specific.”
  • Collect before students leave. Review before next class.

2 Expected Student Difficulties

2.1 1. Confusing GEE with GLMM

The confusion: Both handle clustered/repeated-measures data and both can use non-normal link functions. Students often use the terms interchangeably.

How to address: - Key distinction: GEE models the marginal mean (population-averaged effect); GLMM models the conditional mean (cluster-specific effect). - Example: “The effect of burn treatment on tick counts across all plots (GEE) vs. within a specific plot (GLMM).” - Analogy: GEE is like asking “on average, do treated fields have fewer ticks?” GLMM asks “for plot #7 specifically, do treated conditions reduce ticks?”

Relevant worksheet prompt: Question 6.

2.2 2. Sandwich SEs vs. Model-Based SEs

The confusion: Students don’t understand why you’d use robust SEs instead of the model-based ones — “aren’t model-based SEs better if the model is correct?”

How to address: - In practice, the working correlation is never exactly right. Sandwich SEs are consistent regardless. - If the working covariance is correctly specified, sandwich and model-based SEs converge. - Analogy: Model-based SEs are like a GPS that assumes perfect roads; sandwich SEs are like a GPS that recalculates based on actual traffic.

Relevant worksheet prompt: Question 5.

2.3 3. Negative Binomial vs. Poisson Family Choice

The confusion: Students may not understand what overdispersion means or how to detect it.

How to address: - For count data, Poisson assumes Var(Y) = E(Y). If Var(Y) > E(Y), the data are overdispersed. - Signs of overdispersion: residual deviance/df >> 1 in a Poisson GLM; estimated dispersion parameter > 1. - geepack::geeglm uses a quasi-Poisson approach (scale parameter estimated from data); true negative binomial GEE requires additional packages.

Relevant R script: materials/gee_example.R section “Overdispersion Check.”

2.4 4. Not Recognizing the Cluster Structure

The confusion: Students may identify individual tick observations as the unit of analysis, or confuse the cluster (plot) with the treatment (burn regime).

How to address: - Draw the data structure on the board: Plot 1 (BB) → Jan, Feb, Mar, ... Dec 2010, Jan, ..., Dec 2011 Plot 2 (BB) → Jan, Feb, Mar, ... Dec 2010, Jan, ..., Dec 2011 ... Plot 21 (UBUB) → ... - Key point: plots are the independent units; monthly observations within a plot are dependent (correlated over time).

Relevant entry ticket field: Cluster_ID_and_time_structure.

2.5 5. Working Correlation Choice (AR(1) vs. Exchangeable)

The confusion: Students may think exchangeable is “fine” because GEE is robust to misspecification.

How to address: - GEE estimates are consistent regardless of working correlation — but efficiency (precision) depends on how close the working correlation is to the truth. - Monthly tick counts likely have autocorrelation that decays with lag — AR(1) is more ecologically realistic. - For a 24-time-point monthly series, assuming equal correlation between January and December (exchangeable) as between consecutive months is probably wrong.

3 Tips for Facilitating Presenter–Student Interaction

3.1 Managing the Hot-Seat

  • Rehearse this the day before by telling the presenter: “At about minute 20, I’ll call a Hot-Seat. Groups will ask you a question. You’ll have 30 seconds per question. Don’t panic — say what you know.”
  • Frame it as professional development: “This is what happens at conference Q&A and dissertation defenses.”
  • If a group’s question is unclear or unfair, rephrase it: “I think what they’re asking is…”
  • If the presenter gives a great answer, say so explicitly: “That was a really precise answer — did everyone catch what they said?”

3.2 Managing Challenge Questions from Groups

  • Encourage groups to write genuinely challenging questions, not softballs. During the worksheet phase, if you see a group writing an easy question, push back: “What’s a question that would make a methodologist sweat a bit?”
  • After the presenter responds, ask the questioning group: “Does that answer satisfy you? Anything you’d add?” This creates a mini-dialogue.

3.3 If the Presenter Doesn’t Know the Answer

  • Don’t rescue immediately. Wait 5–8 seconds.
  • Offer: “What would you need to look at to figure that out?”
  • If truly stumped, open to the class: “Who wants to help?”
  • Return to the presenter for a synthesis: “Based on what the class just said, how would you revise your answer?”

4 Follow-Up Assignments

4.1 Option A: R Modification (recommended)

Students run materials/gee_example.R with modifications: 1. Change the correlation structure from "exchangeable" to "ar1" and "independence". Compare QIC values. Which fits best? 2. Add a month*burn_regime interaction term. Does it improve fit? How would you interpret it? 3. Write a 200-word methods paragraph suitable for a journal manuscript describing the GEE you ran.

4.2 Option B: Apply to Own Data

Students identify a dataset from their own research (or a publicly available one) that has: - Count or rate outcome - Repeated measures or clustering

They fit a GEE using geepack::geeglm, justify their choice of family, link, and correlation structure, and write a 300-word methods + results paragraph.

4.3 Option C: Peer Review Practice

Students use activities/rubric_peer_review.md to review a classmate’s Option A or B submission.

5 Contingency Plans

5.1 If the presentation runs long (presenter at 30+ min at 0:40)

  • Interrupt politely: “Let’s stop there — we need to move to the methods lecture. You can post your remaining slides on Canvas.”
  • Skip the Hot-Seat Q&A if it hasn’t happened yet, or fold it into the group challenge questions.

5.2 If the presentation runs short (presenter done at 0:25)

  • Open the floor to the class for general questions (3–4 min).
  • Move into the GEE lecture earlier; use the extra time to go deeper on the estimating equation derivation.

5.3 If the worksheet activity runs short (groups done in 5 min)

  • Prompt deeper thinking: “Group 2, you said exchangeable is fine — convince me. What would the correlation matrix look like for months 1 vs 2, and months 1 vs 24?”
  • Ask groups to peer-review another group’s challenge question.

5.4 If the worksheet activity runs long (groups not done at 10 min)

  • Call time at 10 min regardless. Say: “We’ll only share the challenge questions — skip the written answers for now.”
  • Assign written answers as homework.

5.5 If R code doesn’t work (technical failure)

  • Show the pre-run output in solutions/expected_answers_ticks_gee.md.
  • Walk through the code on the projector without executing it.
  • Send materials/gee_example.R via email/Canvas for students to run at home.
